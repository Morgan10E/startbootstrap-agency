Ethical Summary

Which ethical frameworks support these actions?

Which do not?

  • Deontology

    Duty-Bound

    From the deontological perspective, if tech companies are fulfilling their duty, then their actions are ethically justified. This begs the question: what is their duty? What should be the highest priority for a company in the Cloud? Protecting the environment? Providing reliable services for their users? Ensuring the health of workers near their facilities? If their first priority is their users, then deontology would support their actions. If not, then it would condemn them.

  • Utilitarianism

    Greatest Good for the Greatest Number

    From the utilitarian perspective, if the actions of tech companies have a net positive effect, then their actions are ethically justified. The question is then what are the consequences of their actions? Do they create enough happiness? Can the environment itself be happy or sad due to the damage caused? What if you consider the sadness in the future caused by damage to the environment now? If the happiness of the users outweighs the distress of the environment and the communities around the data centers, then utilitarianism would support the actions of these companies. If not, then it would attack their actions.

  • Cyberlibertarianism?

    Leave tech free from government influence

    Cyberlibertarianism is the idea that many in Silicon Valley have that the government should stay out of the internet, that the internet should remain a free space, a virtual free market. All aspects of the internet, including the physical data centers of the Cloud, have remained highly unregulated. There is no government body in place to monitor data centers, and they remain highly secretive. In fact, the government itself has very little knowledge of its own data centers. With the cyberlibertarian mindset, many tech companies can think that the government should stay out of the internet, even the physical manifestations of the internet. From this mindset, companies can feel justified in everything - even violating clean air regulations, because the rules shouldn’t apply to them. The environmental impact of their actions isn’t even on their radar - only profits.

Getting away from cyberlibertarianism and towards utilitarian or deontological views could protect the environment - we need the companies to start asking themselves the questions we posed above, rather than immediately thinking of themselves.

Low Utilization

Wasting Energy

Data centers are run at 100% around the clock, regardless of demand, to protect against a sudden surge of users slowing down the systems. This results in these centers wasting at least 90 percent of the energy they pull off the grid. A diagnosis of LexisNexis servers revealed that three-quarters of sampled servers were using ten percent of their computational power1. This is not considered unusual for the industry. If a data center is sluggish or shuts down, it can cost someone their career. Even though utilization up to 96.4 percent has been proven possible2, few are willing to risk the transition.

Source: Power, Pollution, and the Internet

In one particular case, energy usage was used as a weapon. A Microsoft data center in Quincy, WA had overestimated their energy usage and was subject to a $210,000 fine. Rather than pay the fine, Microsoft began running giant heaters for no purpose in order to burn energy before the deadline. If they could get within their prediction margin, they would have incurred $70,000 in energy costs, but would have saved $140,000 overall. Microsoft informed the utility that this would be their course of action unless they saw a reduction in the fine. The utility board ultimately reduced the fine to $60,000.

Source: Data Barns in a Farm Town, Gobbling Power and Flexing Muscle

1. LexisNexis used the diagnosis to reduce their energy usage. After updating their hardware and consolidating the work to fewer servers, they reduced the data facility from 25,000 square-feet into 10,000 square-feet.
2. The National Energy Research Scientific Computing Center reached 96.4% utilization by queuing up demanding processes and scheduling them so the machines are running as close to maximum capacity as possible, 24 hours a day.

Ethical Perspectives

Deontological

Do companies have a duty to provide reliably fast service to their customers? Or do companies have a duty to protect the environment, or at least not be wasteful in their energy usage? If companies’ first responsibility is to their customers, then one can argue that keeping their servers running at all times is ethically justified. However, if companies’ first responsibility is to be energy efficient, then their actions are ethically wrong. If they have a duty to achieve some combination of reliable service to their users and energy efficiency, then companies have a moral obligation to increase their utilization. From the deontological perspective, Microsoft’s energy wasting would never be justified.

Utilitarian

The utilitarian perspective has many factors to consider. Is the low utilization better for companies, since it lowers risk and they don’t have to invest in improvements? Or would higher utilization be better, since it would lower energy costs? Does the wasting of energy ultimately subtract from overall happiness, since environmental damage is bad for health? Can the happiness of the environment itself be considered a factor? Does the happiness of the company outweigh the happiness of the utilities or the environment? Depending on how one answers these questions, the low utilization and Microsoft’s actions may or may not be supported by the utilitarian perspective.

Cooling

Water and Fans

With cooling "accounting for approximately 37 percent of electricity usage within a well-designed data center", it's natural place to look at minimizing energy usage. Currently, most corporations use one of two methods CRACs or CRAH's. CRACs (computer room air conditioners) tower server rooms and simply chill the air to let servers operate at a comfortable temperature. CRAHs (computer room air handlers) operate similarly, but cool servers using chilled water. The chilled water is then pumped through coils and take away the heat from the server rooms.

Sources: Five Strategies for Cutting Data Center Energy Costs Through Enhanced Cooling Efficiency
 Computer Room Air Conditioning Unit (CRAC)
 CRAC vs CRAH

Current cooling methods are inefficient because they use air as the main medium to coolthe servers. Air being a poor conductor of heat means a lot of energy is wasted chilling the air in the CRAC and CRAH units. Liquid immersion technology aims to cut down on energy efficiency be directly chilling servers with cold liquid. However, liquids such as water may lead to fast corrosion of server machinery. Instead, specially engineered mineral oils may be the key to keeping servers operational for longer.

Source: Liquid Cooling Drives down Costs and Improves Efficiency

Corporations are looking to drive down cooling costs through innovative means. Google, in particular, purchased a unique site in Finland for a green and efficient data center. Originally a paper mill, Google sought to take advantage of the naturally cool seawater near the location. Addressing one of the big costs of cooling (chilling the water in CRAH's) brought about its own set of challenges. Unlike regular water, seawater is particularly corrosive. Furthermore, the heated seawater couldn't simply be returned to the sea at such a high temperature without disturbing the surrounding environment. The solution was two-fold. First, titanium plates became the filter of choice with their natural resistance to corrosion. Second, the heated exhaust water would be mixed in with fresh, cool seawater before being returned to the sea. Google's Finland plant is an example of green initiatives taking place in data center cooling.

Source: Hamina, Finland – Data Centers – Google

Ethical Perspectives

Deontological

Since cooling has no effect on the user, companies' only duty is to the environment. As we've seen in the innovations by Google, great steps are being taken to ensure that the environment is not put in jeopardy as they strive towards more efficient, cheaper cooling practices. So long as companies continue to act in such a way, they are ethically justified from the deontological perspective.

Utilitarian

From the utilitarian perspective, neglecting to maintain high standards of environmental friendliness is not ethically justifiable. Since environmental damage will have negative consequences to the surrounding ecosystems now and humanity in the future, the greatest net positive action would be to continue to strive for cleaner cooling practices.

Energy Sources

Everything from solar to coal, batteries to diesel generators

Data centers are now responsible for 2% of global carbon emissions1. The amount of emissions is growing at a rate of 12% per year. While a few companies have committed to renewable energy sources2 for their data centers, many rely heavily on coal-generated power.

Sources: How Dirty is your Data?
 Your Data Is Dirty: The Carbon Price of Cloud Computing

Regardless of where the majority of their energy comes from, all data centers come equipped with batteries, spinning fly-wheels, or diesel generators to provide energy in case of the power grid shutting down. The diesel generators in particular have caused issues with the public and the environment. Even if the power doesn’t go out, the generators still emit exhaust since they regularly have to be tested. The diesel particulates are toxic pollutants, and data centers have increasingly been cited for violating clean air regulations3.

Sources: Data Barns in a Farm Town, Gobbling Power and Flexing Muscle
 Power, Pollution, and the Internet

1. A single data center can take the same amount to operate as a medium-sized town.
2. Apple has committed to powering the iCloud exclusively with renewable energy. Facebook has been increasing its renewable energy usage, with its newest data center in Iowa running 100% off wind power.
3. At least a dozen data centers were cited in Illinois and Virginia for clean air regulations. Amazon had 24 violations in three years, including operating diesel generators without an environmental permit.

Ethical Perspectives

Deontological

From the deontological perspective, the diesel generators would be unethical. Humanity has a responsibility to do no harm if it is within their power to do so, and releasing toxins into the air near other businesses, when there are other options, is unjustified. Relying on coal could also be considered unethical, if the option of renewable energy is available. If it is within their power to do so, companies have the duty to preserve the environment.

Utilitarian

From the utilitarian perspective, the diesel generators would be unethical. Since batteries or fly-wheels could be used in place of the generators, the companies could have backup power without exposing others to toxins, thus increasing overall happiness. From the utilitarian perspective, whether or not these companies rely on coal or renewable energy is debatably justified. If the environment is capable of feeling happiness, then coal is not justified. If it is not, then using coal is not justified by utilitarianism, but it also cannot be attacked from the utilitarian perspective.
